Favorite Movies

The Pursuit of Happyness

Based on the true story, The Pursuit of Happyness is about a father and his young son try to make it on their own, even when the struggles of life take a toll on the both of them. This movie is one of my favorites because it teaches me to never give up in life no matter how much I’ve been put down.


Favorite Movie Quote: You got a dream... You gotta protect it. People can’t do somethin’ themselves, they wanna tell you you can’t do it. If you want somethin’, go get it. Period. ~ Will Smith


Akira
Taken place in the post-apocalyptic city of Neo-Tokyo, a teenage boy must get through a city filled with corrupt politicians, capitalists, and government to try and stop his friend with telekinetic powers from destroying the world. Akira is one of my favorites because it is the most action-packed movie I've seen while also presenting questions about society.

"They were too scared, so they hid it away from the public. They forgot all shame and honor, cast off the civilization and science we had created, and shut the lid of the Pandora's Box they themselves had opened." ~ Colonel Shikishima

2001: a Space Odyssey
2001: a Space Odyssey is a mind-bending movie about a mysterious artificial intelligence robot named Hal 9000 who provides a connection between the past and future. 2001 is one of my favorite movies because it encourages my fantasies about the conundrums associated with space while also posing an important question if we as humans should make computers smarter than us.

"I am putting myself to the fullest possible use, which is all I think that any conscious entity can ever hope to do" ~ Hal 9000

Favorite Books

Astrophysics for People in a Hurry
by Neil DeGrasse Tyson
In the book Astrophysics for People in a Hurry, Neil Degrassi Tyson takes the broad topic of Astrophysics and condenses it into a small easy read book answering major questions around the field. Astrophysics for People in a Hurry is one of my favorite books because Tyson's implementations of humor and simple words into his writing style made it more relatable for me and inspired me to learn more about the field.
"Unstable weapons-grade plutonium was the active ingredient in the atomic bomb that the United States explored over the Japanese city of Nagasaki, just three days after Hiroshima, bringing a swift end to World War II. Small quantities of non-weapons-grade radioactive plutonium can be used to power radioisotope thermoelectric generators for spacecraft that travel to the outer solar system, where the intensity of sunlight has diminished below the level usable by solar panels" (Tyson 132).

Me and Earl and the Dyling Girl
by Jesse Andrews

Me, Earl, and The Dying introduces us to Greg who tries to be invisible in school by keeping a structured schedule and only interacting with his one friend Earl. It wasn't until Greg's mother encourages Greg to befriend a classmate who has Leukemia that Greg finally breaks out of his shell. Me, Earl, and The Dying Girl is one of my favorite books because it reinforces how complicated life is which is why we must be kind to each other because our time on this earth is precious.

"So I was sitting there and the whole time I had this insane wish for Rachel to wake up and just tell me everything she had ever thought, so that it could be recorded somewhere, so that it wouldn't be lost" (Andrews 277).

And Then There Were None
by Agathe Christie

In the book, And Then There Were None , ten strangers are brought to a remote island where, one by one, each person is slowly killed off by an anonymous murderer among them. Playing off of the Ten Little Indians nursery rhyme, the murderer dramatizes the mystery. And Then There Were None is one of my favorite books because Christie's succinct writing style drew me into the plot and reeled me in to unveil the mystery.

"I'm afraid of death... Yes, but that doesn't stop death coming..." (Christie 194).
